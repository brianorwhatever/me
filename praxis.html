Praxis Project Proposal

Secure and Privacy-Aware Digital Provenance Across Layered Ownership Systems
Brian Richter · Aviary Tech · University of Victoria · April 2026

1. Problem and Opportunity

A photographer publishes a limited-edition digital print. Over time, the work is collected, resold, and eventually exhibited online — but no single system can tell the full story of its creation, authenticity, and chain of custody. The photographer’s original signing event is tied to a platform that may disappear. A collector’s proof of ownership lives in a marketplace database. And anyone who wants to verify the work’s history must trust several intermediaries whose authority is never clearly stated.

This scenario illustrates three linked problems in digital provenance today. First, provenance is fragile — a claim of authenticity often depends on infrastructure outside the asset itself. Second, trust assumptions are opaque — users rely on custodians without a clear account of what those actors can and cannot do. Third, privacy is poorly handled — public systems overexpose metadata while private workflows sacrifice auditability.

This praxis addresses those problems by studying how digital provenance, ownership transfer, and selective disclosure can be composed into a single layered protocol.

2. Proposed Praxis

The proposed praxis extends the Originals protocol — an existing multi-layer digital asset provenance system built by Aviary Tech — into a research-grade cryptographic systems project organized around layered ownership states. The concrete artifact is a protocol implementation comprising an SDK, DID-method libraries, a Bitcoin inscription layer, and a privacy-aware credential engine, evaluated against explicit security and privacy criteria.

The protocol is organized into four layers, each corresponding to a distinct class of trust requirement:

  • Private creation and local provenance — assets begin in low-cost, offline environments where provenance events can be recorded without publication.
  • Public discovery and selective disclosure — assets become discoverable through web-addressable identifiers while preserving control over which claims become public.
  • High-assurance ownership transfer — assets transition into stronger ownership environments with explicit verification rules.
  • Advanced ownership transitions — constrained shared-ownership or fractional-rights workflows supported as an application layer.

3. Contribution Boundary

The doctoral contribution is the systematic security, privacy, and trust evaluation of a layered provenance architecture — producing the adversary models, privacy tradeoff analysis, structured threat assessment, and empirical evidence that reveal where the architecture’s guarantees hold, where they weaken, and what they cost.

This praxis builds on a substantial existing engineering practice. The Originals protocol implementation comprises over 1,100 commits and already includes a working did:btco resolver and inscriber, verifiable credential issuance and verification, and an initial BBS selective disclosure library. The doctoral contribution begins where the engineering practice becomes the subject of systematic investigation.

4. Research Questions

  • How should a layered provenance protocol be modeled so that transitions between private, public, and high-value ownership states preserve clearly stated security guarantees?
  • Which privacy properties are most valuable in provenance systems, and what cryptographic mechanisms can support them without undermining public verifiability?
  • How can provenance claims, ownership records, and selective disclosure be composed so that users can audit authenticity without exposing unnecessary information?

5. Methodology

The project proceeds in four phases.

Phase 1: Security Model and Provenance Core

Formalizes the protocol structure for creation, discovery, and ownership transfer. Defines participants, adversaries, trust assumptions, and security goals. Produces a reference specification, verification invariants, and an implementation baseline.

Phase 2: Privacy-Aware Provenance

Integrates privacy-preserving techniques into provenance workflows. The primary mechanism is BBS selective disclosure, enabling holders to derive proofs that reveal only selected credential attributes while preserving verifiability. Evaluates how selective disclosure composes with provenance claims across layer transitions.

Phase 3: Ownership Transition and Shared Rights

Examines how the protocol supports constrained ownership partitioning or governance-based transfer scenarios. Includes comparative trust analysis of specific operator constraints against a trusted-operator baseline. Intentionally bounded so the dissertation remains viable if only a constrained prototype is completed.

Phase 4: Evaluation

Produces the empirical and analytical evidence needed for a strong praxis outcome. Includes STRIDE-based threat analysis with at least 15 specific threat scenarios, adversarial test suites, benchmark comparisons across deployment paths, and comparison against existing provenance systems.

7. Deliverables

  • A layered digital provenance protocol specification
  • A reference implementation and verification toolkit
  • A privacy-aware provenance design and evaluation
  • A bounded prototype for advanced ownership transitions
  • Performance, threat, and privacy analyses with defined comparison baselines
  • A DEng praxis dissertation integrating the architecture, implementation, and evidence

8. Expected Contribution

The main contribution of this praxis is a practical framework for secure and privacy-aware digital provenance across multiple ownership layers — independent of any single platform, application, or commercial deployment. Whether or not Aviary Tech or Codex continue to operate, the protocol architecture, trust analysis, privacy evaluation, and open-source implementation remain a reusable contribution to engineering knowledge.

The strongest outcome is not merely a new application. It is a reusable architecture, implementation, and evidence base whose value does not depend on a specific commercial context.
